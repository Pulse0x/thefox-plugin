# Staying Safe During Your Job Search

Job searching involves sharing personal information, clicking links from unfamiliar sources, and interacting with companies you don't yet know. That creates real opportunities for scammers. This page explains what to watch out for and how theFox is designed to help protect you.

---

## What theFox Does to Protect You

**Your personal information stays on your computer.**
Your resume, contact details, work history, and everything you share with the Resume Wizard is saved locally — on your machine, in the `resume/` folder. TheFox never sends your name, email, phone number, or resume content to any external website or service.

**Job searches are anonymous.**
When theHunt searches for job listings, it sends anonymous queries (like "senior product manager remote") — nothing that identifies you. No personal data goes out.

**Suspicious content is filtered.**
TheHunt scans every job listing for signs of manipulation — content designed to trick Claude into behaving in unexpected ways. Anything that triggers those filters gets excluded from your results and flagged to you.

## What theFox Cannot Protect You From

No tool can make job searching completely risk-free. Here are the real threats to know about:

### Fake Job Listings

Scammers post fake jobs on real platforms — including LinkedIn, Indeed, and Glassdoor. These listings look real but are designed to collect your resume, personal information, or money.

**Signs a listing might be fake:**
- The job description is vague, full of errors, or sounds too good to be true
- The salary is unusually high for the role and experience level
- The company name is slightly off from the real company (e.g. "Amazzon" or "Google LLC International")
- There are no specific requirements, just buzzwords
- The listing was posted recently by a brand-new account

**What to do:** Search for the company directly and check their official careers page. If the job isn't listed there, it may not be real.

---

### Phishing Apply Links

Clicking "Apply" on a listing doesn't always take you to the company's real website. Scammers create fake careers pages that look legitimate but are designed to steal your information.

**Before clicking any apply link:**
- Look at the URL before clicking. Hover over the link (on desktop) to see where it actually goes.
- The domain should match the company. `careers.google.com` is real. `google-careers-apply.com` is not.
- If the URL uses a link shortener (bit.ly, tinyurl, ow.ly), be cautious — you can't see where it's going.

**On the apply page itself:**
- The URL in your browser's address bar should show the company's real domain
- Look for `https://` at the start (the padlock icon) — it means the connection is encrypted, though it does **not** mean the site is legitimate
- If something looks off — fonts are wrong, the logo looks stretched, or the page feels unpolished — trust that instinct and close the tab

---

### Resume Harvesting

Some websites pose as job boards but exist only to collect resumes. They show you listings that may not be real jobs and ask you to upload your resume or create an account to "apply."

**Signs of a resume harvesting site:**
- You can't see the job details without creating an account
- The site asks you to upload your resume before showing you any listings
- There's no clear information about who runs the site
- The listings are generic and not specific to real companies

**What to do:** Stick to job boards you recognize (LinkedIn, Indeed, Glassdoor, the company's own careers page) and be cautious about any site that asks for your resume before you've found a role you actually want.

---

### Requests for Personal or Financial Information

Legitimate employers will never ask for the following during the application process:

- Your Social Security number or national ID number
- Your bank account or routing number
- Payment of any kind ("equipment deposit," "background check fee," "training materials")
- Your passport or ID documents before you've received and accepted an offer

If a job asks for any of these things before you've been hired, it's a scam. Stop immediately and do not provide the information.

---

### Off-Platform Communication

If a recruiter or hiring manager asks you to move the conversation off the job board and onto WhatsApp, Telegram, or a personal email address early in the process, be cautious. Legitimate recruiters usually communicate through company email addresses or the platform's messaging system.

It's not always a scam — some real recruiters do use WhatsApp for convenience. But combined with other red flags (vague listing, unusually high pay, pressure to move fast), it's a warning sign.

---

### Pressure and Urgency

Scammers create artificial urgency to stop you from thinking carefully. Phrases like "we need to fill this role immediately," "you must respond within 24 hours," or "we're ready to make an offer right now" are sometimes used to pressure you into sharing information before you've had a chance to verify anything.

Real employers understand that candidates need time to research the company and consider an offer. Anyone who pressures you to skip that step is not acting in your interest.

---

## If Something Feels Wrong

Trust your instincts. If a listing, a recruiter, or an apply page feels off — even if you can't explain why — pause and verify before doing anything.

**What you can do:**
- Ask me to take a second look at a specific listing or URL
- Search for the company independently and check their official site
- Search "[company name] + scam" or "[job title] + scam" to see if others have reported problems
- Report suspicious listings to the platform they appeared on

**Reporting links:**
- LinkedIn: https://www.linkedin.com/help/linkedin/answer/a1340819
- Indeed: https://support.indeed.com/hc/en-us/requests/new
- Glassdoor: https://help.glassdoor.com/s/article/Report-a-Job

Reporting protects other job seekers who may not have the same safeguards in place.

Claude — the AI powering theFox — has access to your conversation, your resume file, and your application tracker. It does not have access to the rest of your computer, your browser history, your other files, or your other accounts unless you explicitly share them.
